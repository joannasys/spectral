The Problem

The intensive care unit (ICU) is a specialised facility focused in treating critically ill patients who require intensive monitoring and complex medical system support.The key goal of the ICU is to stabilise and treat patients with life-threatening conditions and offer peaceful death for patients with unsalvageable conditions.

The development of methods for prediction of mortality rates in Intensive Care Unit (ICU) has been motivated by the need make ICU care more cost effective by:

  • Increasing treatment effectiveness
  • Efficient use of resources
  • Functional discharge to post-acute care facilities
    •
and still provide precise and best outcome for critically ill patients.

Traditionally, hospitals have been using the critical illness severity assessment scores such as Simplified Acute Physiology score (SAPS-I) and Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) Score to access the critical extent of the patientâ€™s condition and predict mortality. The scores assist healthcare providers in prioritising use of resources and providing better patient-specific treatment.

The aim of this project is to develop a better solution for patient-specific prediction of in-hospital mortality that serves as a decision support for medical practitioners and medical care givers.

Data

The data used in this project is obtained from the PhysioNet/CinC Challenge 2012. It consists of records from 4,000 ICU stays. All patients were adults who were admitted for a wide variety of reasons - cardiac, medical, surgical, or trauma. Up to 42 variables were recorded at least once during the first 48 hours:

In-hospital mortality will be our target in this project: 1 being in-hospital death while 0 being survival.

Feature Engineering

Each patient record is in the form of a time series - variables may be observed once, more than once, or not at all in some cases within 48 hours. Descriptor variables are recorded at time 00:00. To capture the best property of the time series, I have extracted out batches of aggregated information from it. The table below shows the original time series data.




I have chosen to extract the mean, minimum and maximum, first and last observed data to represent the average and extremities of the time series. The table below shows the extracted data for a patient record.




After extraction, I observed that most patient records lack laboratory test results and most variables leading to many â€˜Not-a-numberâ€™ (NaN) values in the dataset which would cause a problem in our modelling.

To resolve the issue of NaN values, I imputed the NaN values for each variable of each patient based on the median of the same age group, ICU type and gender group. This seems to better represent the patientâ€™s condition. Certain conditions may have some extremes in certain variables, it would not be fair if I were to perform a simple mean or median imputation.

Data Standardisation

After preprocessing, I used sklearnâ€™s RobustScaler to standardise the numerical variables. Standardisation is essential to improve the predictive performance of our algorithms - especially so for gradient-based classifiers. RobustScaler scales the data based on the percentiles rather than the mean and standard deviation, it is not influenced by marginal outliers.

For categorial features, we used pandasâ€™ pd.get_dummies method to obtain a matrix of dummy variables in 0 or 1. While some algorithms (such as decision trees and random forest) do not require this step, most machine learning algorithm requires all features to be numerical.

It is written in a class to allow easy implementation of sklearnâ€™s convenient Pipelines and FeatureUnion.

Imbalanced Class Resampling

Recall that we are dealing with an imbalanced dataset in this project. There are only 15.0% of in-hospital mortality (positives) in the dataset.



If I were to build a classifier in this dataset, I will probably obtain a 85% accuracy. However, the â€˜correctly classified dataâ€™ would all come from the majority class. This is because most algorithms do not perform well with imbalanced datasets. It will tend to be bias towards the majority class.

To deal with this problem, there are two approaches that we have used in this dataset: Oversampling with Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE) and Edited Nearest Neighbours (ENN).

The SMOTE technique creates synthetic data on the minority class by finding the K-nearest neighbour (KNN), choses a random â€˜neighbourâ€™ and create a similar datapoint as the â€˜neighbourâ€™ randomly.

ENN removes datapoint if the datapointâ€™s class is different from the majority of it K nearest neighbours.

After resampling, we will increase the percentage of minority class from 15.0% to 38.8%.
